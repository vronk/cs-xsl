Urban, sedentary, high prestige variety

General

The Egyptian varieties of Arabic form a quite diversified linguistic group. They are usually subdivided into four major subgroups: the dialects of Upper Egypt, Middle Egypt, Lower Egypt and those spoken in the Oases. The dialect of the capital belongs to the northern group.

By contrast to most other varieties of vernacular Arabic, the dialect of Cairo has been reduced to writing as well. There exists quite a significant amount of literary output, in particular stage plays.

Research History

The first serious reliable descriptions of Egyptian Arabic were written in the 19th century: Wilhelm Spitta published his grammar in 1880 (which can be read online at the Internet Archive) and Vollers a textbook in 1890 (English translation 1895, available at the Internet Archive). These were followed by Nallino (1900) (full text at the Internet Archive) and Willmore (1905) (full text at the Internet Archive).

The great bulk of publications was written after World War II. Interesting information was provided by Mitchell (1956), Tomiche (1962) and Salib (1981). Most studies followed rather traditional approaches. An exception is Wise’s transformational grammar (1975). Another very informative monography is Parkinson’s socio-linguistic study on terms of address in Egyptian Arabic (1985).

The most substantial scholarly contributions to our knowledge of the Egyptian dialects were made by the German dialectologists Behnstedt and Woidich. Most of their work was done in the 80’s and 90’s of the 20th century. Woidich’s grammar (2006) of Cairo Arabic is the so far most comprehensive in-depth study of this variety.

Text Books

For generations of learners of spoken Egyptian the course material compiled by Abdel-Massih (1978-9) was the main guidance. A more up-to-date textbook is Woidich/Heinen’s Kullu tamām! (2004). Furthermore, a graded series of textbooks by Samia Louis has to be mentioned. In these books, all the reading material is presented in the Arabic script. In addition, they come with high quality CDs.

Two German textbooks should also be highlighted: Woidich’s Ahlan wa Sahlan (2002) actually predates his Kullu tamām!. The sesond one is Ahmed’s Lehrbuch des Ägyptisch-Arabischen (1981) which unfortunately contains countless typos. Nevertheless, the texts collected there are very helpful, especially for advanced learners.

Dictionaries

There exist several dictionaries for those who want to study Egyptian Arabic. The hitherto most comprehensive one is Hinds/Badawi (Arabic-English). It was published in 1986.
