Baghdad

Official name بغداد Baġdād
Stephan Procházka

Typology

Eastern › Mesopotamian › Iraqi gǝlǝt (gilit)

Urban Bedouin dialect with significant sedentary substrate, high-prestige variety

Research History

The dialect of the Iraqi capital can be regarded as the prestige variety of the whole country. It is frequently used in the media and widely understood not only in Iraq, but also in the adjacent Gulf region, in Eastern Syria, and in the Iranian province of Khuzestan. The linguistic setting of Baghdad is special for its so-called “communal dialects”. The current prestige variety (described here) is actually the dialect which until fairly recently was spoken solely by the Muslim population. The Christian and Jewish (no longer in Iraq) inhabitants of Baghdad speak a very different dialect of the so-called qǝltu type, which is much closer to Northern Iraq and not influenced by Bedouin features.

The first comprehensive descriptions of the Arabic dialect of Baghdad did not appear before the 1960s. Before that only some very brief remarks (Jeannier 1888, Massignon 1912) and a few textbooks and glossaries were published, particularly Van Ess 1917 (second edition 1938, many reprints until 1989) and van Wagoner 1949, but both contain a mixture of various Iraqi dialects and cannot be regarded as dialectological descriptions. The first scholarly monographs published were Malaika 1963 and Erwin 1963, shortly followed by Blanc 1964. Blanc, who coined the term “communal dialects”, presented a rather concise but excellent description of Muslim, Christian, and Jewish Baghdadi. Abu-Haider 1991 deals with the Christian variety. Palva 2009 convincingly argues that the Bedouin (= gǝlǝt type) character of present-day Muslim Baghdadi is mainly the result of a process of bedouinization during the past 300 years. Thus, for a considerable time after the Mongol sack of Baghdad in 1258, the dialect of Baghdad had remained to be an urban Mesopotamian type dialect that has only slowly changed due to immigration from the countryside. In spite of its importance, Baghdad Arabic has not been intensively studied (compared to, e.g., Cairo or Damascus) and almost all reference works date back to the 1960s.
